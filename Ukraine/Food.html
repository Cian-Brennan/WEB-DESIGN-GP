Borshch

Iconic and symbolic to Ukrainian cuisine since at least the 14th century, Borshch is a hearty beet soup, getting it's deep red from them. It's made from cabbage, potatoes, carrots, onions, tomatoes, sometimes beans or meat, and is traditionally served with sour cream and sprinkled with fresh dill or garlic. Every region has it's own version and it has become a symbol of Ukrainian hospitality and family gatherings.

Varenyky

Traditional Ukrainian dumplings, made of soft dough and filled with various ingredients such as mashed potatoes, cottage cheese, cherries, cabbage, or minced meats. They are boiled and often served with sour cream, fried onions, or butter. Sweet varenyky with cherries are especially popular in summer. Historically, they were prepared for holidays, weddings, and harvest celebrations. They have become symbolic of comfort and home-style cooking as well as the moon due to their crescent shape.

Holubtsi

Holubtsi are cabbage rolls filled with a mixture of rice and minced meat, sometimes barley or mushrooms. They are wrapped in boiled cabbage leaves and simmered in tomato sauce or sour cream.
Historical background: The dish has roots in Eastern Europe and became especially popular in Ukraine in the 18th and 19th centuries. Traditionally, holubtsi were prepared for Christmas, weddings, and family celebrations. In rural Ukraine, they were a sign of prosperity because cabbage and grains were widely available. The name “holubtsi” (literally “little pigeons”) likely came from a similar dish in Turkey that used pigeon meat.

Chicken Kyiv

Chicken Kyiv is a well-known Ukrainian dish consisting of chicken breast filled with cold herb and garlic butter, then rolled, breaded, and fried until crispy. When cut open, the melted butter flows out, creating a dramatic presentation.
Historical background: Although there are debates about its origin, many historians agree that Chicken Kyiv has strong connections to Ukrainian culinary tradition. It gained fame when it was served at the Continental Hotel in Kyiv in the early 20th century. In Soviet times, the dish became popular in international restaurants as a symbol of Ukrainian cuisine. Foreign guests visiting Kyiv often ordered it as a “signature” dish.
